Discover Cumberland's Wildlife

Explore the diverse ecosystem of Georgia's largest barrier island, home to over 300 species of birds, feral horses, and endangered sea turtles.

Cumberland Island Wild Horses

The feral horses of Cumberland Island are the most iconic residents, with a population of about 150-200 horses. Descendants of domesticated horses brought to the island in the 18th century, they now roam freely across beaches, marshes, and maritime forests.

Mammals

Featured

White-tailed Deer

These graceful creatures are common throughout the island, especially in the maritime forest. Dawn and dusk are the best times to spot them.

Fun Fact: During summer, deer's coats turn reddish-brown, while winter coats are more grayish.

Nine-banded Armadillo

These armored mammals are often spotted digging for insects in the undergrowth. They're relatively new to the island, having arrived in the 1970s.

Fun Fact: Armadillos can hold their breath for up to six minutes and cross small streams by walking underwater.

Raccoons

These clever and adaptable mammals are common throughout Cumberland Island, particularly around campsites and near the shore.

Fun Fact: Cumberland Island raccoons have learned to hunt for ghost crabs on the beach at night, showing their remarkable adaptability.

Birds

Bald Eagle

Cumberland Island is home to several nesting pairs of America's national bird. Look for them soaring over the beach or perched in tall pines.

Fun Fact: Cumberland's eagles build some of the largest nests in the world, with some measuring up to 10 feet across.
Featured

Wood Stork

These distinctive wading birds are endangered and can be spotted in the island's marshes and pond areas, especially during low tide.

Fun Fact: Wood storks are the only stork species that breed in the United States, with Cumberland Island serving as an important habitat.

Painted Bunting

One of North America's most colorful birds, these vibrant songbirds can be spotted in brushy areas and forest edges during spring and summer.

Fun Fact: Male painted buntings are nicknamed "nonpareils" (meaning "without equal") because of their extraordinary coloration.

Reptiles & Amphibians

Featured

Loggerhead Sea Turtle

These threatened sea turtles nest on Cumberland's beaches from May to August. Hatchlings emerge at night and make their way to the sea.

Fun Fact: Female loggerheads return to the same beach where they were born to lay their eggs, navigating across thousands of miles of ocean.

American Alligator

These impressive reptiles can be found in Cumberland's freshwater ponds and occasionally in brackish marshes. They're typically shy around humans.

Fun Fact: Alligators have been around for 200 million years, surviving the extinction that killed the dinosaurs.

Eastern Diamondback Rattlesnake

The largest venomous snake in North America lives in Cumberland's pine forests. They're rarely seen and prefer to avoid humans.

Fun Fact: Eastern diamondbacks can detect temperature changes as small as 0.003°C through special heat-sensing pits near their eyes.

Conservation & Wildlife Safety

Cumberland Island National Seashore is committed to protecting its diverse wildlife. As a visitor, you play a crucial role in conservation efforts and ensuring these animals remain wild and healthy.

Keep Wildlife Wild

Never feed or approach wild animals. Feeding wildlife damages their natural behaviors and can be dangerous.

Maintain Safe Distances

Stay at least 50 feet away from horses and deer, and 100 feet from alligators. Use binoculars for closer views.

Protect Sea Turtle Nests

Stay away from marked sea turtle nests. At night, avoid using flashlights on the beach during nesting season.

Pack In, Pack Out

Take all trash with you when you leave. Even biodegradable items can take years to decompose and attract wildlife.
