Trail Guide

Cumberland Island National Seashore offers over 50 miles of diverse hiking trails and roads that traverse maritime forests, interior wetlands, historic districts, marsh ecosystems, and pristine beaches.

Trail Map

Map Source: National Park Service

For an interactive map and additional details, visit the National Park Service website.

Popular Trails on the Southern End

Dungeness Roadways

Difficulty: Easy

A self-guided walk through the Dungeness Historic District, offering views of the historic ruins and surrounding landscape.

Trail Highlights

  • • Historic Dungeness ruins
  • • Oak trees draped in Spanish moss
  • • Wildlife viewing opportunities

River Trail

Difficulty: Easy

A short walk along the water's edge between Dungeness Dock and Sea Camp, offering scenic views of the marsh and river.

Trail Highlights

  • • Marsh views
  • • Bird watching opportunities
  • • Connects Dungeness Dock to Sea Camp

Nightingale Trail

Difficulty: Easy to Moderate

Provides views from within the maritime forest, offering a peaceful walk through the island's interior.

Trail Highlights

  • • Maritime forest views
  • • Wildlife viewing
  • • Shaded walking path

Parallel Trail to Little Greyfield Road

Difficulty: Moderate

A walk through the trees, across the dunes, and back down the beach, offering diverse landscapes and views.

Trail Highlights

  • • Forest and dune ecosystems
  • • Beach access
  • • Diverse wildlife viewing

Hiking the Southend

This area allows visitors to experience oak trees draped in Spanish moss, the Dungeness Historic District, boardwalks over marsh and sand dunes, and stretches of undeveloped beach. Several intersecting trails and roads are present; a map and trail signs help in navigation.

Amenities

  • • Four restrooms with drinking fountains
  • • Trail maps and signs for navigation
  • • Boardwalks over marsh and sand dunes
  • • Beach access points

Hiking the Northend and Wilderness

Hiking north on the Parallel Trail or Main Park Road takes visitors through live oak canopies, palmetto forests, pine stands, open fields, tidal creeks, freshwater wetlands, and lakes. The Wilderness boundary is 5 miles north of the Sea Camp dock and is best explored when camping or private boating.

Important Information

  • • Drinking water available at Stafford Beach Campground and Plum Orchard Mansion
  • • Other water sources require treatment
  • • Remote area requires adequate preparation
  • • Inform someone of your itinerary before hiking

Trail Safety Tips

  • Always carry plenty of water and snacks
  • Wear appropriate footwear and clothing
  • Be aware of your surroundings and watch for wildlife
  • Stay on marked trails to protect the environment
  • Check weather conditions before starting your hike
  • Let someone know your hiking plans
  • For northend hiking, prepare adequately and inform others of your itinerary
  • Treat water from natural sources before drinking
